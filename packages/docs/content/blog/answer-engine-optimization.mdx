**Answer engine optimization (AEO)** is the practice of structuring, publishing, and earning content so that AI answer engines — like ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google's AI Overviews, and Gemini — cite your brand in the answers they generate. Where traditional SEO competes for a click on a results page, AEO competes to be the source the AI quotes.

**Key takeaways**

- AEO optimizes for being *cited inside an AI-generated answer*, not for ranking a blue link a user clicks.
- Answer engines pick sources by relevance, authority, structure, and freshness, then ground their answer in what they retrieve.
- The fundamentals overlap with SEO, but AEO adds extractable formatting, entity clarity, and structured data.
- Much of AEO is invisible unless you measure it. Tracking which prompts mention your brand — and which actually cite it — is part of the discipline.
- "AEO," [generative engine optimization (GEO)](/blog/what-is-generative-seo), and "LLMO" describe largely the same practice.

## What is answer engine optimization?

An *answer engine* is any system that responds to a question with a synthesized answer instead of a list of links. Ask ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google's AI Overviews a question and you get a paragraph — often with a handful of cited sources — rather than ten blue links to evaluate yourself. Answer engine optimization is the work of making your brand one of those cited sources.

The term emerged alongside AI search in 2023–2024 as a counterpart to SEO. It is still a young, loosely-defined label, and you will see it used interchangeably with generative engine optimization (GEO) and LLM optimization (LLMO). The distinctions are mostly emphasis, not substance: all three describe the same shift in how people find information and how brands earn visibility within it.

That shift matters because the unit of visibility has changed. In classic search, visibility means a ranking — position three for a keyword. In an answer engine, there often is no ranking to hold. There is one answer, assembled on the fly from sources the model decided to trust, and your brand is either named in it or it is not. A user may never see, let alone click, the page that informed the answer. The citation *is* the visibility.

AEO does not replace SEO. The two share a foundation — useful content, technical health, topical authority — and many AEO wins are downstream of good SEO, because the same pages that rank well are often the ones answer engines retrieve. What AEO adds is a layer of optimization aimed specifically at retrieval, extraction, and citation by machines rather than ranking and clicking by humans.

## AEO vs SEO: what's different

SEO and AEO answer two different questions. SEO asks, "How do I rank this page so a person clicks it?" AEO asks, "How do I become the source an AI cites when it answers this question?" The table below maps the practical differences.

| Dimension | Traditional SEO | Answer engine optimization (AEO) |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Rank a page in the results list | Be cited inside the generated answer |
| Unit of visibility | Position for a keyword | A mention or citation in an answer |
| User action | Click through to your site | Often reads the answer, no click |
| Primary signals | Links, content, technical health | Relevance, authority, structure, freshness |
| Content shape | Pages that satisfy a query | Passages that are easy to extract and quote |
| Measurement | Rankings, impressions, clicks | Mentions, citations, share of voice across prompts |
| Where it shows | Google, Bing results pages | ChatGPT, Perplexity, AI Overviews, Gemini |

The overlap is real: an authoritative, well-structured page is good for both. The divergence is in emphasis. AEO rewards content that a model can lift a clean, correct sentence from, and it rewards being mentioned across the wider web in ways that build the model's confidence that your brand is the right answer.

## How answer engines choose what to cite

Most answer engines work by *retrieval-augmented generation*: when you ask a question, the system retrieves candidate sources from a search index or live web fetch, then generates an answer grounded in those sources and cites some of them. Understanding that pipeline tells you where to intervene. Four factors consistently shape which sources get cited.

**Relevance.** The source has to match the meaning of the prompt, not just its keywords. Models match semantically, so content that addresses the actual question — in plain language, with the question and answer close together — is easier to retrieve and quote.

**Authority and trust.** Engines lean toward sources they have reason to trust: established sites, pages with corroborating mentions elsewhere, and brands with a consistent, verifiable identity. This is why off-site reputation matters as much as on-page work.

**Structure.** A model can extract a clean answer from a clear heading, a short direct paragraph, a list, or a table far more reliably than from a wall of prose. Extractable structure is one of the highest-leverage, most controllable AEO factors.

**Freshness.** For anything time-sensitive, engines favor recently updated sources. Stale facts — old pricing, deprecated features, last year's numbers — get passed over or, worse, repeated incorrectly.

You cannot see the retrieval index, and you cannot directly edit an answer. What you can do is make your content the most relevant, trustworthy, well-structured, and current option for the questions that matter to your brand.

## How to optimize for answer engines

AEO is a discipline, not a trick. The work breaks into seven concrete moves.

**1. Answer the question directly, early, and in plain language.** Lead each page with a tight, self-contained answer to the question it targets — ideally 40–60 words that stand on their own. This is the passage most likely to be lifted into an answer. Bury the answer under a throat-clearing intro and you forfeit the citation.

**2. Structure for extraction.** Use descriptive, question-shaped headings ("How does X work?"). Break key points into lists. Put comparisons in tables — models extract tabular data well. Keep paragraphs short. The goal is that any single section can be quoted without the rest of the page for context.

**3. Establish entity clarity.** Make it unambiguous who you are, what you do, and how you relate to the topics you cover. Use consistent naming and facts across your site and the wider web, and mark up your organization with structured data so engines can resolve your brand as a distinct entity rather than guessing.

**4. Earn mentions on sources answer engines trust.** Citations are partly a popularity-and-corroboration game. Being referenced — accurately — in roundups, reviews, documentation, and reputable third-party content raises the odds a model treats your brand as a credible answer. This is the AEO analogue of link building, and it is covered in depth in our guide to [earning AI citations](/blog/ai-citations).

**5. Add structured data.** `FAQPage`, `Article`, `Organization`, and `HowTo` schema give engines explicit, machine-readable structure. Schema is not a magic ranking lever, but it removes ambiguity and makes your content easier to parse and quote.

**6. Keep facts current.** Date your content, update it when reality changes, and remove stale claims. Freshness is both a retrieval signal and an accuracy safeguard — you do not want an engine confidently repeating a number you changed six months ago.

**7. Monitor, then iterate.** AEO without measurement is guesswork. Track which questions surface your brand, which cite it, where competitors are named instead, and how answers describe you. Then feed those gaps back into your content. The next section covers how.

## How to measure AEO performance

The hardest part of AEO is that the "results page" is different for every user and leaves no public record. You cannot open an incognito tab and check your rank. Instead, you measure AEO by sampling: run the prompts your audience actually asks, across the engines they use, repeatedly, and record what comes back.

A useful AEO measurement framework tracks four things:

- **Mentions** — how often your brand appears in answers for relevant prompts.
- **Citations** — how often an answer links to or sources your site specifically.
- **Share of voice** — your presence versus competitors for the same prompts.
- **Accuracy and sentiment** — whether answers describe your brand correctly and favorably.

Doing this by hand does not scale past a handful of prompts, which is why brands use dedicated tooling to run prompt sets on a schedule and chart the trend. Our walkthrough on [how to track your brand in AI search](/blog/track-brand-ai-search) covers the method step by step, and [Elmo](https://www.elmohq.com/) is an open-source tool built to automate exactly this measurement loop across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and AI Overviews.
