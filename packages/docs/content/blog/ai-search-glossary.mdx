AI search introduced a new vocabulary, and much of it overlaps or competes. This glossary defines the terms in plain English, notes where they mean the same thing, and links to fuller guides. Start with the [answer engine optimization](/blog/answer-engine-optimization) pillar if you want the concepts in context, or read how [AI is changing SEO](/blog/ai-for-seo) for the bigger picture. Otherwise, use the groups below as a reference.

## Core disciplines

These three terms describe the same emerging practice — earning visibility inside AI answers — from slightly different angles.

**[Answer engine optimization (AEO)](/blog/answer-engine-optimization)** — The practice of structuring and publishing content so AI answer engines cite your brand in the answers they generate. Where SEO competes for clicks on a results page, AEO competes to be the source an AI quotes.

**[Generative engine optimization (GEO)](/blog/what-is-generative-seo)** — Optimizing content to be surfaced and cited by generative AI search tools. GEO is a near-synonym for AEO that emphasizes the *generative* nature of the engines.

**LLMO / LLM SEO** — Large language model optimization, also called LLM SEO: optimizing content so large language models — and the AI tools built on them — mention and cite your brand. Used interchangeably with AEO and GEO. (Fun fact: the name *Elmo* comes from LLMO.)

## Where AI answers appear

**[AI search engine](/blog/best-ai-search-engine)** — A tool that answers a query with a synthesized, often-cited response instead of a list of links — for example Perplexity, ChatGPT Search, or Google's AI Overviews.

**Answer engine** — Any system that responds to a question with a direct answer rather than a list of sources to evaluate. AI search engines are answer engines; the term now usually refers to them.

**[AI Overviews](/blog/google-ai-overviews)** — Google's AI-generated summaries shown above traditional search results for many queries, built on its Gemini models and linking to sources.

## What to measure

**AI visibility** — How present and accurately represented a brand is across AI answers: how often it is mentioned, how often it is cited, and how it is described. Measuring and improving it is the focus of [AI visibility software](/ai-visibility-tools).

**[Citation (in AI search)](/blog/ai-citations)** — A link or source reference an AI answer attributes information to. Citations are the unit of visibility in AI search: being cited can send traffic and signals that the engine trusts your content.

**Prompt volume** — The number of distinct prompts, or questions, you track across AI engines. AI visibility tools meter usage by prompt volume, and broader coverage gives a more reliable read on your presence.

**Share of voice (AI)** — The share of relevant AI answers that mention your brand versus competitors. A higher AI share of voice means engines name you more often than rivals for the prompts that matter.

## How the engines work

**Entity** — A distinct, identifiable thing — a brand, person, product, or concept — that search and AI systems recognize and relate to others. Clear, consistent entity information helps engines resolve and trust your brand.

**Structured data (schema)** — Machine-readable markup, using schema.org vocabulary, that labels the meaning of content: an article, FAQ, organization, or how-to. It helps engines parse and reuse your content accurately.

**Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)** — A method where an AI system retrieves relevant sources, then generates an answer grounded in them. Most AI search engines use RAG, which is why being retrievable and quotable is central to AEO.

**Grounding** — Tying an AI's answer to specific external sources so it reflects real, current information rather than the model's memory alone. Grounded answers are likelier to cite the sources they used.

**Hallucination** — When an AI generates confident but false or unsupported information. For brands, hallucinations can mean inaccurate claims about your product — one reason to monitor how AI describes you.

## AEO vs GEO vs LLMO — are they the same?

For practical purposes, yes. Answer engine optimization, generative engine optimization, and LLM optimization all describe the work of getting your brand surfaced and cited inside AI-generated answers. The differences are emphasis, not substance:

- **AEO** stresses the *answer* — the single response that replaces a list of links.
- **GEO** stresses the *generative* engines doing the answering.
- **LLMO / LLM SEO** stresses the *large language models* underneath.

You will see all three used as synonyms across the industry, and none should change what you actually do. If you are building a strategy, pick one term for internal consistency and focus on the shared playbook: answer questions directly, structure content for extraction, build entity authority, earn trusted citations, and measure your results.
